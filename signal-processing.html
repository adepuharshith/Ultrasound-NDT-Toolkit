Core techniques for extracting meaning from ultrasonic waveforms

Technique 01

Cross-Correlation

Time-of-Flight Pulse-Echo TOFD Matched Filter

In pulse-echo UT you often receive two echoes — a reference (front wall) pulse and a back wall (or defect) pulse — and need to measure the time delay Δt between them precisely to size the reflector.

Simply reading the peak positions works for clean signals, but noise and waveform distortion make that unreliable. Cross-correlation slides one pulse over the other, computes their overlap (inner product) at every lag, and returns a correlation function whose peak sits exactly at Δt — even for noisy, clipped, or overlapping echoes.

Rxy(τ) = ∫ x(t) · y(t + τ) dt
τ = lag; peak of Rxy gives Δt → depth = v·Δt / 2
1
Capture the A-scan with two separated echoes (FW = reference, BW = target).
2
Window the back-wall echo as the search template y(t).
3
Slide y(t) across the full signal x(t), computing the overlap at each lag τ.
4
Find peak of Rxy(τ) — its position is the best estimate of Δt.
5
Compute depth: d = v · Δt / 2 (factor of 2 for round-trip).

Why correlation beats peak-picking: Noise shifts an individual peak unpredictably. The correlation sums contributions over the entire pulse shape — making the peak location robust even at SNR < 10 dB. In TOFD, where lateral-wave and tip-diffraction echoes overlap, cross-correlation is essential for sub-sample Δt accuracy.

In practice: cross-correlation can be computed efficiently via FFT as Rxy = IFFT( FFT(x)* · FFT(y) ), turning an O(N²) sliding-dot-product into O(N log N). Most UT instruments and MATLAB's xcorr() / Python's scipy.signal.correlate() implement exactly this.

Technique 02

Wavelet Transform & Time–Frequency Analysis

A raw A-scan tells you what frequencies are present (via FFT) but not when they arrive. For broadband pulses, dispersive media (plates, composites), or overlapping modes, that distinction matters enormously.

The FFT gives global frequency content — a flat average over the whole signal window. It cannot tell you that low frequencies arrived early and high frequencies arrived late, which is exactly what happens in dispersive Lamb wave propagation.

Enter the Wavelet Transform

Instead of sines (which extend to ±∞), the Continuous Wavelet Transform (CWT) uses a mother wavelet — a short, localized oscillating function — and slides it across the signal at every time position and every scale (∝ 1/frequency). The result is a 2-D map: time on one axis, frequency on the other.

W(a, b) = (1/√a) ∫ x(t) · ψ*((t − b)/a) dt
a = scale (∝ 1/f), b = time shift, ψ = mother wavelet (Morlet used here)

The Morlet wavelet is a Gaussian-windowed complex sinusoid — ideal for UT because its shape closely matches a bandlimited ultrasonic pulse, giving excellent time–frequency resolution balance.

FFT vs. Wavelet — When to use which

FFT
Best for stationary signals where frequency content doesn't change over time. Fast (O(N log N)), gives exact frequency values. Use for: bandwidth characterization, transducer spectrum, pulse spectrum.
Wavelet CWT
Best for non-stationary signals: dispersive waves, multi-mode Lamb, AE transients. Reveals when each frequency arrives. Use for: Lamb wave mode separation, dispersion curve extraction, AE source characterization.
