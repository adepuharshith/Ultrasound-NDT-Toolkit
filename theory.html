🌀 Wave Propagation

Ultrasonic waves are mechanical disturbances that propagate through a medium by transferring kinetic and potential energy between neighboring particles. In NDE, frequencies typically range from 0.1 MHz to 50 MHz, well above the audible limit.

🌏 Live simulation — longitudinal wave & particle motion

Orange arrow = instantaneous particle velocity vₚ on the highlighted particle — oscillates back and forth, magnitude varies sinusoidally.  Cyan arrow = wave (phase) velocity c — the pattern always advances to the right at c = fλ, independent of vₚ.  Colour bands: blue = compression (particles crowded), orange = rarefaction (particles spread).

Wave Equation

The scalar wave equation governing pressure in a fluid:

∇²p − (1/c²) ∂²p/∂t² = 0

where p is acoustic pressure and c is the wave speed.

Wave Modes in Solids

  • Longitudinal (P-wave): particle motion parallel to propagation. Fastest mode; exists in all media.
  • Shear (S-wave / transverse): particle motion perpendicular to propagation. Only in solids; slower than P-waves (cS ≈ 0.55 cL).
  • Rayleigh (surface) wave: elliptical particle motion confined to the surface; useful for surface defect detection.
  • Lamb waves (plate waves): guided waves in thin plates; symmetric (S) and antisymmetric (A) modes; highly dispersive.

Wave Speed in Isotropic Solids

cL = √[(E(1−ν)) / (ρ(1+ν)(1−2ν))]
cS = √[G/ρ] = √[E / (2ρ(1+ν))]

where E = Young's modulus, ν = Poisson's ratio, ρ = density, G = shear modulus.

Typical values: Longitudinal speed in steel ≈ 5900 m/s; in aluminum ≈ 6320 m/s; in water ≈ 1480 m/s; in HDPE ≈ 2500 m/s.

Wavelength and Resolution

λ = c / f

Spatial resolution is fundamentally limited to approximately one wavelength. Higher frequency → smaller wavelength → finer resolution, but also more attenuation. This is the central design trade-off in UT inspection.

🔄 Acoustic Impedance

Acoustic impedance Z is a material property that determines how much of an incident wave is reflected or transmitted at an interface.

Z = ρ × c    [units: Pa·s/m = Rayl]

Reflection & Transmission Coefficients (Normal Incidence)

R = [(Z2 − Z1) / (Z2 + Z1)]²     (intensity reflection)
T = 1 − R     (intensity transmission)
Example: Steel–air interface: Zsteel ≈ 46 MRayl, Zair ≈ 0.0004 MRayl → R ≈ 100%. Nearly all energy reflects — this is why couplant is essential.

Importance of Couplant

A thin liquid couplant (water, gel, oil) between the transducer and test piece eliminates the air gap, enabling energy transmission. Without couplant, the transducer–air–solid interface reflects nearly all acoustic energy back.

🔴 Attenuation

Wave amplitude decreases with propagation distance due to absorption (conversion to heat) and scattering (energy redirected by grain boundaries, pores, inclusions).

A(x) = A0 · e−α·x

or in dB: Loss (dB) = αdB · f · x

αdB is the attenuation coefficient in dB/(mm·MHz); it is material- and frequency-dependent and must be measured experimentally for each material.

Frequency Dependence

  • Absorption: scales roughly linearly with frequency (viscous losses).
  • Rayleigh scattering (d ≪ λ): scales as f⁴ — dominant in fine-grained materials at high frequency.
  • Stochastic scattering (d ~ λ): scales as f² — typical grain-noise regime in metals.
Rule of thumb: For carbon steel, α ≈ 0.5–2 dB/(mm·MHz) at 5 MHz depending on grain size. Coarse-grained castings can be 10× worse.

🔌 Transducers

Ultrasonic transducers convert electrical energy to acoustic energy (and back) via the piezoelectric effect. The active element is typically a piezoelectric crystal (PZT, PVDF, LiNbO₃) that deforms when a voltage is applied.

Piezoelectric Effect

When mechanical stress is applied to a piezoelectric material, an electric charge is generated (direct effect). Conversely, applying voltage produces mechanical deformation (converse effect). NDE transducers exploit both: transmit (converse) and receive (direct).

Transducer Types

  • Contact transducers: used directly on the test surface with couplant. Available as straight-beam (longitudinal) or angle-beam (shear/Rayleigh).
  • Immersion transducers: water-coupled; allow precise focal control and scanning. Common for C-scan imaging.
  • Dual-element (TR) transducers: separate transmit and receive elements; reduced near-surface dead zone; useful for corrosion mapping.
  • EMAT (Electromagnetic Acoustic Transducer): non-contact; generates waves via Lorentz force; no couplant needed; lower sensitivity.
  • Air-coupled transducers: non-contact, used for composites and foam. Low impedance mismatch managed by matching layers.

Bandwidth & Pulse Shape

A transducer's −6 dB bandwidth determines axial resolution. Broadband transducers (80–120% fractional bandwidth) give short pulses and fine axial resolution. Narrowband transducers sacrifice resolution for sensitivity.

🔭 Beam Physics

Near Field (Fresnel Zone)

In the near field, the beam intensity varies irregularly due to constructive/destructive interference. The near-field distance (also called Rayleigh distance) is:

N = D² / (4λ) = D² · f / (4c)

where D = transducer diameter, λ = wavelength. Inspection is generally more reliable in the far field (beyond N), or at the natural focus.

Far Field (Fraunhofer Zone)

Beyond the near field, beam diverges with a half-angle:

sin(θ½) ≈ 0.514 λ / D    (−6 dB edge)

Focused Transducers

A lens or curved element focuses the beam at a depth F. The −6 dB focal spot diameter governs lateral resolution:

dspot ≈ 1.02 λ F / D = 1.02 F / (f-number)
Design trade-off: Tighter focus (smaller f-number) → better lateral resolution, but shorter depth of field. For thick parts, an unfocused or weakly focused beam may be preferred.

📈 Snell's Law & Mode Conversion

At an oblique interface between two media, refraction and mode conversion occur. The generalized Snell's law (for all mode combinations) is:

sin(θi) / ci = sin(θr) / cr

Critical Angles

  • First critical angle: refracted longitudinal wave travels at 90°; only shear wave transmitted.
  • Second critical angle: refracted shear wave travels at 90°; total internal reflection (surface/Rayleigh waves generated).
Angle beam inspection: Wedge transducers exploit mode conversion at the wedge–part interface to inject shear waves at a specific angle (e.g., 45°, 60°, 70°) for weld inspection.

🤖 Imaging Modes

A-scan

The most basic display: amplitude vs. time. Shows echoes from interfaces and defects along the beam axis. The time axis is converted to depth using d = c·t/2 (round-trip).

B-scan

A 2D cross-section: the transducer is scanned linearly, and A-scans are stacked to form a depth profile. Resembles a medical ultrasound image.

C-scan

A planar top-view map: the transducer is raster-scanned over the part surface, and a gated amplitude (or TOF) value is recorded at each XY position. Widely used for composite inspection and corrosion mapping.

SAFT (Synthetic Aperture Focusing Technique)

Post-processing algorithm that computationally focuses pulse-echo data from a single-element transducer. Each A-scan contributes to a spatially coherent image at every depth point. Equivalent to SAR in radar.

FMC / TFM (Full Matrix Capture / Total Focusing Method)

With a phased array, every transmit–receive element combination is recorded (Full Matrix). TFM then delay-and-sums this data to focus at every pixel in the image, achieving near-optimal resolution everywhere. The current gold standard for advanced array imaging.

TOFD (Time of Flight Diffraction)

Uses diffracted signals (not reflected) from defect tips. Two angled transducers straddle a weld; diffraction at crack tips allows precise depth sizing independent of orientation. Excellent for vertical planar defects.

📺 Signal Processing

Envelope Detection (Hilbert Transform)

The RF signal from a transducer is a modulated sinusoid. The envelope (magnitude of the analytic signal) is computed via the Hilbert transform and used for display and gating.

Filtering

Bandpass filtering removes out-of-band noise and DC offset. A typical filter is centered at the transducer's center frequency with a bandwidth matching the transducer's −6 dB bandwidth.

Time Gain Compensation (TGC)

Depth-dependent amplification applied to compensate for attenuation. A TGC ramp keeps the displayed signal level roughly constant with depth.

Pulse Compression

Transmitting a long coded pulse (chirp) and applying matched filter at reception increases SNR (processing gain) while maintaining range resolution. Used in air-coupled and low-SNR applications.

SNR & Noise Sources

  • Electronic noise: amplifier Johnson noise floor.
  • Coherent grain noise (grass): scattering from microstructure; coherent with signal; hardest to suppress.
  • Geometric noise: surface roughness, part geometry echoes.

🔈 Phased Arrays

A phased array transducer contains many small elements (typically 16–256) that can be excited with programmable time delays, enabling electronic beam steering and focusing without physical movement.

Beam Steering

tn = (n · d · sinθ) / c

Applying delay tn to element n steers the beam to angle θ. Pitch d must be ≤ λ/2 to avoid grating lobes.

Sectorial Scan (S-scan)

The most common phased-array display: the beam is electronically swept over a range of angles (e.g., 40°–70°) at a fixed focal depth, creating a fan-shaped image. Used extensively for weld inspection.

Linear Scan (E-scan)

A fixed aperture (subset of elements) is electronically stepped along the array while keeping the same focal law. Equivalent to mechanically scanning a single-element probe but much faster.

Key advantage of phased arrays: Electronic scanning is orders of magnitude faster than mechanical scanning, enables rapid volumetric coverage, and allows real-time image reconstruction at inspection speed.

🔬 Advanced Techniques

Nonlinear Ultrasound

At high acoustic amplitudes, material nonlinearity generates harmonics (2f, 3f …) of the fundamental frequency. The acoustic nonlinearity parameter β is sensitive to early-stage fatigue damage, dislocations, and microcracking — well before linear methods detect anything.

Guided Wave Inspection

Long-range inspection of pipes and plates using Lamb / SH waves. A single transducer ring can screen tens of meters of pipe for corrosion. Mode selection and frequency tuning are critical to maximizing sensitivity for a given defect type.

Photoacoustic / Laser UT

A pulsed laser generates broadband ultrasound via thermoelastic expansion (non-contact transmitter). Detection can use a laser interferometer (LDV/SLDV) or an air-coupled transducer. Enables inspection of hot, moving, or inaccessible surfaces.

Acoustic Microscopy

High-frequency (50 MHz–2 GHz) focused ultrasound for microstructural imaging and sub-surface feature detection in electronics, biomaterials, and thin coatings. Resolution can reach sub-micron at GHz frequencies.
