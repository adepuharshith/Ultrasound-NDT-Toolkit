Before diving into ultrasound, it is essential to understand what makes sound fundamentally different from light and radio waves. The animations below contrast a longitudinal pressure wave (sound) with an electromagnetic wave (light, radio, X-ray) — two completely different physical phenomena.

Why sound wins for metal inspection: EM waves (radio, microwave, visible light) are stopped dead at any metal surface — the free electrons reflect them completely. Where Each Wave Is Used

The physical differences between sound and EM waves lead to very different application domains. Both are essential tools in science and engineering — just for completely different jobs.

🔊 Sound Wave — Applications
  • Ultrasonic NDT/NDE — flaw detection in metals, welds, and composites; the core use case for this toolkit
  • Medical ultrasound — prenatal imaging, cardiac echocardiography, abdominal scans (soft-tissue B-mode)
  • Sonar — underwater mapping, submarine detection, fish-finding, seafloor bathymetry
  • Acoustic emission (AE) — real-time monitoring of crack growth and structural damage
  • Ultrasonic cleaning — cavitation removes surface contamination from precision parts and surgical instruments
⚡ EM Wave — Applications
  • X-ray radiography & CT — imaging internal casting defects, bone fractures, and security screening
  • Radar & GPS — aircraft navigation, weather detection, speed enforcement, autonomous vehicles
  • WiFi, 5G & satellite — wireless communications and global positioning across any environment
  • Infrared thermography (IRT) — surface hot-spot detection in electronics, composites, and building envelopes
  • Optical fiber — high-bandwidth data transmission via light pulses in glass fiber

Fundamental Principle of Ultrasonic NDT

In pulse-echo UT, a transducer fires a short burst of ultrasound into the part and listens for returning echoes. The time it takes each echo to arrive directly encodes depth — flaws appear as early echoes, the back wall gives total thickness. Everything flows from d = c × t / 2.

What Does Ultrasound Actually Measure?

Three distinct material properties are encoded in a single pulse-echo A-scan — each read from a different feature of the returned waveform.

01

Wave Speed

Sound travels at a characteristic speed set by the material's elastic stiffness and density. Measuring the round-trip time between the front-wall (FW) and back-wall (BW) echo gives wave speed directly — no destructive contact needed beyond the transducer.

c = 2d / Δt

d = known thickness  ·  Δt = FW-to-BW time difference
Steel ≈ 5 920 m/s  ·  Aluminium ≈ 6 320 m/s

📊 A-scan — Time of Flight

FW = front-wall echo  ·  BW = back-wall echo  ·  Δt = round-trip transit time

02

Attenuation

Sound loses energy to absorption (heat) and scattering as it propagates. The back-wall echo arrives weaker than the transmitted pulse — the amplitude ratio over the round-trip path gives the attenuation coefficient α.

α = (1/2d) · ln(AFW / ABW)

Units: Np/m or dB/mm  ·  Higher α → shorter inspection range
Typical: 0.1–5 dB/mm at 5 MHz in metals

📊 A-scan — Amplitude Loss

ABW is smaller than AFW due to material attenuation — the decay is exponential with depth

03

Backscatter

In polycrystalline metals, grain boundaries scatter sound in all directions. A fraction returns to the transducer as random “grain noise” filling the A-scan between FW and BW. Its intensity is a direct measure of grain size and microstructure.

BSC ∝ f⁴ · d³

Rayleigh regime (grain size d « λ)  ·  Coarser grains + higher frequency → stronger noise

🎗 Material — Grain Microstructure

Orange flashes = grain boundary scatter events as wave pulse traverses the material

📊 A-scan — Grain Noise

Random grain noise fills the region between FW and BW — amplitude ∝ grain size × f⁴

"Ultrasound" simply means sound above the human hearing limit of ~20 kHz. For industrial NDE, the useful range extends from 0.1 MHz to 50 MHz — frequencies far beyond anything audible.

The fundamental trade-off: Resolution is limited to approximately one wavelength (λ = c / f). Higher frequency → shorter wavelength → finer resolution. But higher frequency also means more attenuation — the wave dies out sooner. A 5 MHz probe in steel gives λ ≈ 1.2 mm; at 1 MHz, λ ≈ 5.9 mm. Choose frequency based on the smallest flaw you need to detect vs. the depth you need to reach.

How is Ultrasound Generated?

Ultrasound for inspection is generated by a transducer — a device that converts electrical energy into mechanical vibration (and back again). The key mechanism is the piezoelectric effect.

The Piezoelectric Effect — Simply Put

Certain crystals (quartz, PZT ceramic) have a special property: squeeze them and they produce a voltage; apply a voltage and they squeeze themselves. When a short electrical pulse is applied to a piezoelectric element, it vibrates rapidly — generating a burst of ultrasound. When a returning echo hits the element, it produces a tiny voltage that the instrument records. The same element transmits and receives.

The Pulse-Echo Principle

In most NDE setups, the transducer fires a short burst of ultrasound and then listens for echoes. Reflections return from:

  • The front surface (initial pulse / entry echo)
  • Any internal flaws (defect echo)
  • The back wall of the part (back-wall echo)

The time between sending and receiving each echo, multiplied by the wave speed and divided by 2 (round-trip), gives the depth of each reflector.

Scanning Methods — A-scan

The A-scan (Amplitude scan) is the most fundamental display in ultrasonic testing. It shows amplitude vs. time for a single transducer position — essentially what the transducer "hears" after it fires a pulse.

The time axis is converted to depth using d = c × t / 2 (round-trip). Three key features appear:

  • Initial pulse (IP): The transmitted burst — appears at t = 0.
  • Defect echo: A reflection from an internal flaw — appears at a time proportional to the flaw's depth.
  • Back-wall echo (BW): Reflection from the far surface — appears at a time proportional to the total thickness.

A-scans can be displayed as RF (raw bipolar), full rectified (both halves folded positive), or envelope (smooth amplitude via Hilbert transform). Most field instruments default to rectified RF.

A-scan formula: d = c × t / 2    where d = depth, c = wave speed (material-specific), t = measured time-of-flight. The factor of 2 accounts for the round-trip path (down and back).

Scanning Methods — B-scan

A B-scan (Brightness scan) builds a 2D cross-section image by moving the transducer linearly across the surface and stacking A-scans side by side. The horizontal axis is scan position; the vertical axis is depth.

B-scans are excellent for showing flaw depth and through-thickness extent. They are commonly used for corrosion mapping, weld root inspection, and in-service thickness profiling.

Limitation: A large near-surface reflector can "shadow" smaller, deeper reflectors in the same beam path because the beam cannot see past a strong reflector. For complex geometries, multiple scan directions or phased-array volumetric methods are preferred.

Scanning Methods — C-scan

A C-scan (Plan view scan) produces a top-down 2D map of the part. The transducer is raster-scanned in X and Y, and a gated amplitude (or time-of-flight) value is recorded at each position. The result looks like an aerial photograph of the internal structure.

The color (or grayscale) at each pixel represents either:

  • Gated amplitude: Peak amplitude within a depth gate — bright means strong reflection (likely a flaw or delamination).
  • Time-of-flight (TOF): The depth of the first reflector within the gate — used for thickness mapping and contour following.

C-scans are widely used for composite inspection (delamination, impact damage), corrosion mapping, and production-line quality control. They require automated data acquisition — typically computer-controlled immersion scanning or a squirter system.

Summary of the three scanning methods:
A-scan = what echoes exist at this one spot (amplitude vs. time).   B-scan = how deep is the flaw along a line (cross-section view).   C-scan = where is the flaw across the whole surface (map view).
Advanced Topics

🔄 Acoustic Impedance

Acoustic impedance Z characterises how a material resists the propagation of pressure waves. At any interface between two media with different Z values, the wave splits into a reflected wave and a transmitted wave — the greater the mismatch, the more energy is reflected and the less penetrates. This principle governs everything from couplant selection to echo amplitude interpretation.

$$Z = \rho\,c \qquad [\text{Pa·s/m} = \text{Rayl}]$$

Reflection & Transmission Coefficients (Normal Incidence)

For a wave striking an interface at normal incidence, the intensity reflection and transmission coefficients are:

$$R = \left(\frac{Z_2 - Z_1}{Z_2 + Z_1}\right)^{\!2} \quad (\text{intensity reflection}) \qquad T = 1 - R \quad (\text{intensity transmission})$$

Note that R here is the intensity (power) reflection coefficient. The pressure-amplitude reflection coefficient is the signed quantity r = (Z2 − Z1)/(Z2 + Z1), which is negative when Z2 < Z1 (a “soft” reflection with phase inversion) — a sign change visible in RF-mode A-scans and exploited in half-wave rectification to distinguish crack faces from back-wall echoes.

Worked NDE Example — Immersion Inspection of Aluminium

In immersion UT, a transducer fires through a water path into an aluminium plate. At the water–aluminium entry surface (Z1 = 1.48 MRayl, Z2 = 17.1 MRayl):

$$R = \left(\frac{17.1 - 1.48}{17.1 + 1.48}\right)^{\!2} = \left(\frac{15.62}{18.58}\right)^{\!2} \approx 0.71 \quad (71\,\% \text{ of intensity reflected})$$

Only 29 % of the incident intensity enters the part. On exit through the back wall (aluminium–water), the same mismatch applies — roughly 29 % of the remaining intensity exits into the water where it produces the back-wall echo. This 71 % entry-surface reflection is why the initial-pulse echo is so dominant in an immersion A-scan. Any indication amplitude must be compared against the back-wall echo, not the entry surface, to account for this transmission loss.

Importance of Couplant

A thin liquid couplant eliminates the transducer–air–solid interface (R ≈ 100 % for steel–air) by substituting a medium whose impedance bridges the gap. Water gel (Z ≈ 1.5 MRayl) is not perfectly matched to steel (Z = 46 MRayl), but it reduces the air-gap reflection from ~100 % to ~71 % — a ~12 dB improvement in transmitted amplitude. Specialised impedance-matching layers bonded to the transducer face can increase transmission further by introducing an intermediate Z layer of thickness λ/4, acting as an acoustic transformer.

Phase inversion diagnostic: In RF mode, a reflection from a lower-Z material (e.g. the far face of a steel plate into water, or a gas pore within metal) appears as a polarity-inverted echo relative to the entry surface. This sign flip is a direct consequence of r < 0 and can distinguish an open crack (gas-filled, soft reflection) from a bonded inclusion (hard reflection) — a technique used in diffusion-bonded and brazed-joint inspection.

🔴 Attenuation — Detailed

Total attenuation in a polycrystalline metal has two physically distinct contributions: absorption (thermoelastic and viscous energy conversion to heat) and scattering (energy redirected by grain boundaries, pores, and inclusions). Their frequency scalings differ, so the dominant mechanism — and the optimal inspection frequency — depends on grain size relative to wavelength.

$$\alpha_\text{total}(f) = \underbrace{\alpha_\text{abs} \cdot f}_{\text{absorption}} + \underbrace{\alpha_R \cdot f^4 \cdot d^3}_{\text{Rayleigh}} + \underbrace{\alpha_S \cdot f^2 \cdot d}_{\text{stochastic}}$$

where d is mean grain diameter, f is frequency, and αR, αS are material constants. The active regime is determined by the dimensionless parameter kd = 2πfd/c:

Three Scattering Regimes

Schematic log–log attenuation vs. frequency for a polycrystalline metal. Regime boundary shifts left for coarser grains (larger d).

Regime Summary

RegimeConditionα scalingDominant in
Rayleigh kd ≪ 1  (d ≪ λ/6) α ∝ f⁴ · d³ Fine-grained wrought steel / Al at 1–5 MHz
Stochastic kd ~ 1 α ∝ f² · d Coarse-grained castings, austenitic welds, AM metals
Geometric kd ≫ 1 α → frequency-independent Very coarse grains; rarely used in practice
Absorption All regimes α ∝ f Polymers, viscous media; minor in metals

NDE Example: Austenitic Stainless Steel Welds

Austenitic SS weld metal solidifies as columnar dendritic grains, with effective grain diameters of 50–500 µm and strong crystallographic texture. At a typical 5 MHz inspection frequency in steel (cL ≈ 5 900 m/s, λ ≈ 1.2 mm), a 200 µm grain gives kd ≈ 0.7 — squarely in the stochastic regime. The consequence is severe coherent grain noise (often called backwall grass) that can mask crack tip signals. The standard engineering response is to drop to 1–2 MHz, accepting lower axial resolution, to push kd back into the Rayleigh regime and recover signal-to-noise. The trade-off is quantified by the figure of merit:

$$\text{FOM} = \frac{\alpha}{f^2}$$

FOM is approximately constant within the stochastic regime and is used as a material characterisation parameter for phased-array inspection qualification. For fine-grained carbon steel (d ~ 20 µm), FOM ≈ 0.005–0.02 dB/(mm·MHz²); for coarse austenitic weld metal it can reach 0.1–0.5 — an order-of-magnitude degradation in inspectability.

Practical rule: For carbon steel, α ≈ 0.5–2 dB/(mm·MHz) at 5 MHz depending on grain size. Coarse-grained castings can be 10× worse, and austenitic weld metal 20–50× worse than fine-grained wrought steel. Always measure α on a representative coupon — never assume handbook values for cast or additively manufactured microstructures.

🔭 Beam Physics

Wave Velocity from Material Properties

Sound speed is not a universal constant — it depends on the elastic stiffness and density of the host material. For isotropic solids the two fundamental velocities are:

$$c_L = \sqrt{\frac{E\,(1-\nu)}{\rho\,(1+\nu)(1-2\nu)}} \qquad c_S = \sqrt{\frac{G}{\rho}} = \sqrt{\frac{E}{2\rho\,(1+\nu)}}$$

where E is Young's modulus, G the shear modulus, ν Poisson's ratio, and ρ density. Because steel has ν ≈ 0.29, the ratio cS/cL ≈ 0.55 — shear waves travel at roughly half the longitudinal speed, directly explaining the critical-angle geometry in Snell's law.

Copper1300.348 9604 7002 260

Why this matters for inspection design: A higher cL shortens the wavelength at a given frequency (λ = c/f), improving axial resolution. Conversely, CFRP composites have directional velocities that vary with fibre orientation — a constant velocity assumption introduces depth errors that can exceed 10 % in multi-ply layups. Always verify velocity on a reference block of the same material and lay-up.

Near Field (Fresnel Zone)

In the near field, beam intensity varies irregularly due to constructive/destructive interference. The near-field distance (Rayleigh distance) is:

$$N = \frac{D^2}{4\lambda} = \frac{D^2 f}{4c}$$

Inspection is generally more reliable in the far field (beyond N), or at the natural focus.

Far Field Beam Divergence

$$\sin\!\left(\theta_{1/2}\right) \approx \frac{0.514\,\lambda}{D} \quad (-6\,\text{dB half-angle}) \qquad \sin\theta \approx \frac{1.2\,\lambda}{D} \quad (\text{Rayleigh criterion})$$

Focused Transducers

$$d_{\text{spot}} \approx \frac{1.02\,\lambda F}{D} = \frac{1.02\,F}{f\text{-number}}$$
Design trade-off: Tighter focus (smaller f-number) → better lateral resolution, but shorter depth of field. For thick parts, an unfocused or weakly focused beam may be preferred.

📈 Snell's Law & Mode Conversion

When an ultrasonic wave strikes a planar interface at oblique incidence, it simultaneously produces a refracted P-wave and a mode-converted S-wave in the second medium. Both obey Snell's law independently, each governed by its own wave speed:

$$\frac{\sin\theta_i}{c_{L1}} = \frac{\sin\theta_L}{c_{L2}} = \frac{\sin\theta_S}{c_{S2}}$$

Because cL2 ≠ cS2, the two transmitted beams diverge in direction. In steel (cL = 5900 m/s, cS = 3200 m/s) the refracted P bends nearly twice as far from the normal as the mode-converted S.

Critical Angles

As θi increases, two thresholds mark where a transmitted wave reaches 90° (grazes the interface) and undergoes total internal reflection (TIR):

  • First critical angle (θc1): sin(θc1) = cL1 / cL2. The refracted P-wave undergoes TIR; for θi > θc1, only the mode-converted S transmits into the solid.
  • Second critical angle (θc2): sin(θc2) = cL1 / cS2. The mode-converted S also undergoes TIR; for θi > θc2, both waves are totally reflected and Rayleigh surface waves can be generated along the interface.

Angle-beam probes are designed to operate with the wedge angle strictly between θc1 and θc2, ensuring a single-mode (pure shear) beam enters the test piece.

Interface cL1 (m/s) cL2 / cS2 (m/s) θc1 θc2

Water → Aluminum 1480 6320 / 3130 13.5° 28.2°

NDE example — 45° shear weld probe (Perspex–steel):
Required wedge angle: sin(θwedge) = sin(45°) × cPerspex / cS-steel = 0.707 × 2730 / 3200 = 0.603 → θwedge ≈ 37°.
The wide Perspex–steel window (27.6° – 58.5°) means a single wedge material covers 45°, 60°, and 70° shear angles — each cut at a different face angle. For a 25 mm thick plate, one half-skip leg covers a surface distance of T × tan(45°) = 25 mm; a full V-path spans 50 mm, directly constraining probe placement during weld-root inspection.

Angle Beam Geometry

$$\text{Depth} = \ell_{\text{beam}}\cos\theta_R \qquad \text{Surface dist.} = \ell_{\text{beam}}\sin\theta_R$$

A full skip (V-path off the back wall) traverses a total beam path of 2T / cos(θR), where T is the part thickness and θR is the shear refraction angle. The quarter-, half-, and three-quarter-skip positions interrogate the near-surface, mid-thickness (back wall), and near-far-surface regions respectively — critical for full volumetric weld coverage per ASME and AWS D1.1.

🔈 Phased Arrays

A phased array transducer contains many small elements (typically 16–256) excited with individually programmable time delays, enabling electronic beam steering and focusing without physical movement of the probe. The delay law that produces steering is derived from the condition that wave crests from all elements arrive simultaneously at the desired focal point.

Beam Steering — Delay Law

For a linear aperture with element pitch d and longitudinal wave speed c, the delay applied to element n (zero-indexed) to steer the beam to angle θ is:

$$t_n = \frac{n \cdot d \cdot \sin\theta}{c}$$

Elements on the side toward the desired angle fire last; the progressive delay tilts the plane wavefront. To add a focal depth F, a quadratic term is superimposed:

$$t_n = \frac{n \cdot d \cdot \sin\theta}{c} \;-\; \frac{1}{c}\!\left(\sqrt{F^2 + (n\,d)^2} - F\right)$$

The pitch constraint d ≤ λ/2 is critical: violating it produces grating lobes — spurious coherent secondary beams at angles sinθg = sinθ ± λ/d that generate false indications. For a 5 MHz shear-wave array in steel (cS = 3200 m/s, λ = 0.64 mm), the pitch must be ≤ 0.32 mm — a significant manufacturing constraint that limits aperture size for a given element count.

Sectorial Scan (S-scan)

The beam is stepped electronically through a range of steering angles — typically 40°–70° for weld inspection — at a fixed focal depth or with a depth-corrected focus, producing a fan-shaped cross-sectional image. Each angular step requires recomputing the delay law and firing the array; modern instruments execute this in microseconds, reconstructing a full S-scan image in real time at PRFs above 1 kHz.

In a typical 25 mm butt-weld inspection, a 64-element 5 MHz linear array on a Perspex wedge steps from 40° to 70° shear in 1° increments. A single probe placement interrogates the entire weld cross-section that would previously require three separate contact probes (45°, 60°, 70°). The resulting image directly shows flaw depth and lateral position, substantially reducing inspection time and operator dependency relative to manual A-scan methods.

Full Matrix Capture / Total Focusing Method (FMC/TFM)

In FMC every element transmits in turn while all N elements receive, capturing the full N×N inter-element response matrix. TFM then constructs an image by coherently delay-and-summing that matrix at every pixel (x, z):

$$I(x,z) = \left|\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N} A_{ij}\!\left(t_{ij}(x,z)\right)\right|^2 \qquad t_{ij} = \frac{r_i(x,z) + r_j(x,z)}{c}$$

where ri and rj are the distances from transmitter i and receiver j to the image pixel. Because the focal law is computed post-acquisition, the image is optimally focused at every depth simultaneously — unlike conventional phased-array where focus is fixed at one depth per firing. The penalty is data volume: a 64-element array captures 64×64 = 4,096 A-scans per image frame. Real-time TFM requires GPU acceleration or dedicated FPGA pipelines.

NDE example — additive manufacturing inspection: Laser powder bed fusion (L-PBF) Ti-6Al-4V parts contain sub-millimetre lack-of-fusion pores aligned with the build layers. A 128-element 10 MHz linear array in FMC/TFM mode achieves lateral resolution ≈ λ/2 ≈ 0.15 mm in water immersion, resolving individual pores that are invisible to conventional pulse-echo at the same frequency. The full-aperture focusing also suppresses coherent grain noise from the columnar β-grain microstructure by 8–12 dB relative to a single-focal-depth S-scan, directly improving the probability of detection for sub-critical defects.

📏 Calibration & Reference Levels

Calibration establishes a quantitative reference so that any indication can be evaluated consistently regardless of depth or scan position. The core problem it solves is that echo amplitude from an identical flaw decreases with depth due to beam spreading and attenuation — without a depth-corrected baseline, a deep 3 mm crack looks weaker than a shallow 1 mm pore.

Decibel Scale

UT amplitudes span several orders of magnitude, so all gain and sensitivity comparisons are expressed logarithmically:

$$\Delta\,(\text{dB}) = 20\log_{10}\!\!\left(\frac{A_2}{A_1}\right)$$

The factor of 20 (not 10) arises because acoustic intensity scales as amplitude squared; \(20\log_{10}(A_2/A_1) = 10\log_{10}(A_2^2/A_1^2)\). This means the dB scale is the same whether you express it in terms of pressure amplitude or intensity.

Quick conversions: +6 dB = ×2 amplitude  |  +20 dB = ×10  |  −6 dB = ×0.5  |  −20 dB = ×0.1  |  −40 dB = ×0.01

Distance Amplitude Correction (DAC)

DAC is constructed by scanning reference reflectors of identical size (side-drilled holes, SDH, or flat-bottom holes, FBH) at multiple depths in a calibration block made of the same material as the part under inspection. At each depth the gain is held fixed and the peak echo is marked on the A-scan screen. Connecting these peaks traces the DAC curve — a direct empirical map of how amplitude from an equal-area reflector decays with path length under the combined effects of beam divergence (\(\propto 1/r^2\) in the far field) and material attenuation (\(\propto e^{-2\alpha r}\)). Any indication reaching the DAC curve is flagged for evaluation; most codes add sub-DAC evaluation lines at −6 dB (50 % of DAC amplitude) and −14 dB.

Worked Example — Contact UT on Steel Pressure Vessel

Setup: 5 MHz, 12.7 mm diameter contact transducer on low-alloy steel (cL = 5 900 m/s). Near field N = D²f / 4c = 12.7² × 5×10⁶ / (4 × 5 900) ≈ 34 mm, so reflectors at 25, 50, and 75 mm are all in the far field. An ASTM E1158 block with 2.4 mm (3/32") SDH at those three depths is used.

Time-Corrected Gain (TCG) — the Digital Equivalent

Modern digital instruments implement DAC in hardware as TCG: instead of drawing a reference curve on the A-scan, the instrument dynamically increases amplifier gain with time-of-flight so that the reference echo from any depth appears at the same screen height (typically 80 % FSH). This linearises the display — every point on the A-scan can be compared against a flat 100 % reference line. TCG is mandatory for phased-array instruments and encoded scanning where the gain must track to sub-millimetre depth increments.

  • ASTM E1158: Distance-amplitude blocks — side-drilled holes at increasing depths.
  • ASME Basic Calibration Block: Required for pressure vessel and piping inspection (ASME Sec. V).
  • IIW V1 / V2 blocks: International Welding Institute blocks for velocity measurement and angle-probe index point / refracted-angle verification.

⚡ Pulser-Receiver & the Signal Chain

Every UT system revolves around a pulser-receiver: the instrument that fires the transducer and amplifies the returning echoes. Understanding its settings is essential for optimising sensitivity, resolution, and noise rejection.

Pulser Settings

SettingTypical RangeEffect on Signal
Pulse Energy (Voltage) 100 – 800 V Higher voltage → greater acoustic energy transmitted → deeper penetration, but also more ringing (longer pulse) and potential saturation on strong reflectors.
Damping (Impedance) 50 Ω – 1 kΩ Higher damping shortens the pulse, improving axial resolution at the cost of lower amplitude. Low damping = high energy, longer ring-down. Match to application: thin-wall = high damping; deep penetration = low damping.
Pulse Repetition Frequency (PRF) 10 Hz – 20 kHz Must be low enough that all echoes return before the next pulse fires. Too high a PRF causes "ghost echoes" from the previous pulse. Rule: PRF < c / (2 × max depth).

Receiver Settings

SettingFunction
Gain / Amplification (dB)Scales the entire A-scan. Every +6 dB doubles the displayed amplitude. Used to bring the reference echo to a standard level (e.g., 80% full-screen height).
Rectification ModeSelects how the bipolar RF signal is processed before display — see animation below.
Filter (Bandpass)Removes out-of-band electronic noise. Centre frequency should match the transducer's centre frequency; narrow bandwidth improves SNR but can broaden the pulse in time.
Reject (Suppression)Clips all signals below a threshold level. Removes grass/noise floor but can hide real low-amplitude indications — use with caution.

🖐 TOFD — Time-of-Flight Diffraction

Conventional pulse-echo measures reflection amplitude to size flaws — but amplitude depends on crack orientation, roughness, and coupling, making accurate sizing unreliable. TOFD instead measures the time difference between diffracted signals from the crack tip and crack base, giving a direct geometric measurement of crack height independent of amplitude.

From the geometry of the two right triangles, the crack height is:

$$a = \cos\theta \times \frac{(t_{\text{base}} - t_{\text{tip}})\; v}{2}$$

where θ is the probe half-angle, v is the wave velocity in the material, and the time difference Δt = tbase − ttip is read directly from the TOFD B-scan display. Division by 2 converts round-trip time to one-way path length.

Practical note: TOFD is paired with pulse-echo in modern weld inspections (TOFD gives crack height; pulse-echo gives lateral position). The combination, known as "TOFD + PA", is now standard in nuclear and pressure-vessel inspections per ASME Section V.

🔧 Application — Weld Inspection

Weld inspection is the largest single application of contact UT. A complete weld examination uses a two-stage approach mandated by codes such as AWS D1.1 and ASME Section V.

Stage 1 — Straight-Beam Lamination Scan

A normal-incidence (0°) longitudinal transducer scans the base metal on either side of the weld — the heat-affected zone (HAZ). This detects laminations that would otherwise deflect or absorb the angle beam and cause missed indications in Stage 2.

Stage 2 — Angle-Beam Weld Volume Scan

Refracted shear waves at 45°, 60°, or 70° sweep through the weld cross-section. The inspector scans longitudinally (along the weld) and transversely, moving the probe between the quarter-skip, half-skip, and three-quarter-skip positions to achieve full volumetric coverage.
