1 Longitudinal vs Transverse Waves

All waves transfer energy through a medium without permanently displacing the material itself. The key difference between wave types is how the particles in the medium move relative to the direction the wave is travelling.

🌏 Longitudinal wave — particles oscillate along wave direction

Blue bands = compression (particles crowded)   Orange bands = rarefaction (particles spread).   Particles push and pull in the same direction the wave travels.

🌞 Transverse wave — particles oscillate perpendicular to wave direction

Blue particles = displaced upward   Orange particles = displaced downward.   Particles oscillate perpendicular to the wave's travel direction.

Key Difference — At a Glance

Property Longitudinal (P-wave) Transverse / Shear (S-wave)
Particle motion Parallel to wave direction (back & forth) Perpendicular to wave direction (up & down)
Other names Compression wave, P-wave, pressure wave Shear wave, S-wave
Media it can travel in Solids, liquids, and gases Solids only (no shear stiffness in fluids)
Speed in steel ~5,900 m/s ~3,200 m/s (about 0.55 × longitudinal)
Everyday examples Sound, speech, ultrasound, earthquake P-waves Vibrating guitar string, earthquake S-waves
Simple analogy: Push and pull a Slinky along its length → you get a longitudinal wave (compressions travel along it). Shake a rope up and down → you get a transverse wave. Both carry energy from your hand to the other end, but by very different particle motions.

2 Speed: Sound vs Electromagnetic Waves

How fast a wave travels depends on the wave type and the medium it moves through. Sound (a mechanical wave) and light (an electromagnetic wave) behave very differently.

Speed of Sound in Different Media

Sound needs a medium to travel. The stiffer and denser the material, the faster sound travels — counterintuitively, sound is fastest in solids, not gases.

Sound vs Electromagnetic (EM) Waves

Key insight: Sound is roughly 50,000× slower than light in air. That slowness is actually an advantage for inspection — the time it takes an ultrasonic pulse to travel through a 50 mm steel block (~17 µs) is easily measurable with standard electronics, letting us compute the exact depth of a flaw from time-of-flight (d = c × t / 2).

EM waves (X-rays, microwaves, light) do not penetrate metals, which is why sound — specifically ultrasound — is the preferred wave for inspecting metallic structures.

3 Energy Loss: Longitudinal vs Transverse

As a wave travels through a material, it gradually loses energy. This is called attenuation and comes from two sources: absorption (energy converted to heat) and scattering (energy deflected in all directions by grain boundaries, pores, and inclusions).

Comparing Attenuation

Wave Type Travels in Attenuation in Steel Practical implication
Longitudinal (P) All media ~0.5–2 dB/(mm·MHz) Can penetrate thick sections; couples through liquid couplant
Transverse / Shear (S) Solids only ~1–4 dB/(mm·MHz) — typically 2× higher Higher loss limits inspection range; cannot travel through couplant layer
Surface (Rayleigh) Solid surface only Low in-surface attenuation Useful for surface cracks; depth limited to ~1λ
Why can't shear waves travel in liquids or gases? Liquids and gases have no shear stiffness — their molecules slide past each other freely. A shear wave requires adjacent layers of material to pull on each other sideways, which only happens in solids. This is also why earthquakes S-waves stop at Earth's liquid outer core — a clue that helped geologists discover it is molten!

Attenuation Law

A(x) = A0 · e−α·x

where α is the attenuation coefficient [dB/(mm·MHz)], x is propagation distance, and A0 is the initial amplitude. Attenuation increases with frequency — there is always a trade-off between resolution (higher frequency) and penetration depth (lower frequency).

4 Why Longitudinal Waves for Metal Inspection?

Given that both wave types exist in solids, why do most straight-beam contact and immersion inspections use longitudinal waves? Here are the key reasons:

Reason Explanation
Works through liquid couplant A thin layer of water or gel is needed between the transducer and the metal surface. Only longitudinal waves can travel through this liquid film — shear waves die immediately in the liquid.
Lower attenuation Longitudinal waves attenuate less per unit distance in metals, allowing inspection of thicker sections and detection of deeper flaws.
Simple generation A straight-face transducer naturally generates longitudinal waves. No wedge or angle adapter is needed for most thickness measurements and flaw detection.
Predictable speed Longitudinal wave speed in metals is stable and well-characterised (~5,900 m/s in steel). Time-of-flight to depth conversion is straightforward.
Works in all materials The same approach works in metals, plastics, ceramics, and composites — no limitation to solids only.
When are shear (transverse) waves used? Shear waves are deliberately introduced using wedge transducers for weld inspection and crack detection. Because cracks are typically oriented vertically, a shear wave hitting the crack face at an angle reflects strongly, making them excellent for detecting weld defects. The technique is called angle-beam (or TOFD) inspection. This is an advanced topic — see Snell's Law below.

5 The Ultrasound Spectrum for Inspection

"Ultrasound" simply means sound above the human hearing limit of ~20 kHz. For industrial NDE, the useful range extends from 0.1 MHz to 50 MHz — frequencies far beyond anything audible.

Choosing the Right Frequency

Frequency Range Wavelength in Steel Best for
0.1 – 0.5 MHz 12 – 59 mm Coarse-grained castings, concrete, rough surfaces; high penetration
1 – 5 MHz 1.2 – 5.9 mm Most common NDE range — good balance of penetration and resolution
5 – 15 MHz 0.4 – 1.2 mm Small defects, thin sections, fine-grained metals, composites
15 – 50 MHz 0.12 – 0.4 mm High-resolution imaging, microelectronics, thin coatings
The fundamental trade-off: Resolution is limited to approximately one wavelength (λ = c / f). Higher frequency → shorter wavelength → finer resolution. But higher frequency also means more attenuation — the wave dies out sooner. A 5 MHz probe in steel gives λ ≈ 1.2 mm; at 1 MHz, λ ≈ 5.9 mm. Choose frequency based on the smallest flaw you need to detect vs. the depth you need to reach.

6 How is Ultrasound Generated?

Ultrasound for inspection is generated by a transducer — a device that converts electrical energy into mechanical vibration (and back again). The key mechanism is the piezoelectric effect.

The Piezoelectric Effect — Simply Put

Certain crystals (quartz, PZT ceramic) have a special property: squeeze them and they produce a voltage; apply a voltage and they squeeze themselves. When a short electrical pulse is applied to a piezoelectric element, it vibrates rapidly — generating a burst of ultrasound. When a returning echo hits the element, it produces a tiny voltage that the instrument records. The same element transmits and receives.

The Pulse-Echo Principle

In most NDE setups, the transducer fires a short burst of ultrasound and then listens for echoes. Reflections return from:

  • The front surface (initial pulse / entry echo)
  • Any internal flaws (defect echo)
  • The back wall of the part (back-wall echo)

The time between sending and receiving each echo, multiplied by the wave speed and divided by 2 (round-trip), gives the depth of each reflector.

7 Scanning Methods — A-scan

The A-scan (Amplitude scan) is the most fundamental display in ultrasonic testing. It shows amplitude vs. time for a single transducer position — essentially what the transducer "hears" after it fires a pulse.

The time axis is converted to depth using d = c × t / 2 (round-trip). Three key features appear:

  • Initial pulse (IP): The transmitted burst — appears at t = 0.
  • Defect echo: A reflection from an internal flaw — appears at a time proportional to the flaw's depth.
  • Back-wall echo (BW): Reflection from the far surface — appears at a time proportional to the total thickness.

A-scans can be displayed as RF (raw bipolar), full rectified (both halves folded positive), or envelope (smooth amplitude via Hilbert transform). Most field instruments default to rectified RF.

A-scan formula: d = c × t / 2    where d = depth, c = wave speed (material-specific), t = measured time-of-flight. The factor of 2 accounts for the round-trip path (down and back).

8 Scanning Methods — B-scan

A B-scan (Brightness scan) builds a 2D cross-section image by moving the transducer linearly across the surface and stacking A-scans side by side. The horizontal axis is scan position; the vertical axis is depth.

B-scans are excellent for showing flaw depth and through-thickness extent. They are commonly used for corrosion mapping, weld root inspection, and in-service thickness profiling.

Limitation: A large near-surface reflector can "shadow" smaller, deeper reflectors in the same beam path because the beam cannot see past a strong reflector. For complex geometries, multiple scan directions or phased-array volumetric methods are preferred.

9 Scanning Methods — C-scan

A C-scan (Plan view scan) produces a top-down 2D map of the part. The transducer is raster-scanned in X and Y, and a gated amplitude (or time-of-flight) value is recorded at each position. The result looks like an aerial photograph of the internal structure.

The color (or grayscale) at each pixel represents either:

  • Gated amplitude: Peak amplitude within a depth gate — bright means strong reflection (likely a flaw or delamination).
  • Time-of-flight (TOF): The depth of the first reflector within the gate — used for thickness mapping and contour following.

C-scans are widely used for composite inspection (delamination, impact damage), corrosion mapping, and production-line quality control. They require automated data acquisition — typically computer-controlled immersion scanning or a squirter system.

Summary of the three scanning methods:
A-scan = what echoes exist at this one spot (amplitude vs. time).   B-scan = how deep is the flaw along a line (cross-section view).   C-scan = where is the flaw across the whole surface (map view).
Advanced Topics

🔄 Acoustic Impedance

Acoustic impedance Z is a material property that determines how much of an incident wave is reflected or transmitted at an interface.

Z = ρ × c    [units: Pa·s/m = Rayl]

Reflection & Transmission Coefficients (Normal Incidence)

R = [(Z2 − Z1) / (Z2 + Z1)]²     (intensity reflection)
T = 1 − R     (intensity transmission)
Example: Steel–air interface: Zsteel ≈ 46 MRayl, Zair ≈ 0.0004 MRayl → R ≈ 100%. Nearly all energy reflects — this is why couplant is essential.

Importance of Couplant

A thin liquid couplant (water, gel, oil) between the transducer and test piece eliminates the air gap, enabling energy transmission. Without couplant, the transducer–air–solid interface reflects nearly all acoustic energy back.

🔴 Attenuation — Detailed

Attenuation is frequency-dependent and must be measured experimentally for each material.

Frequency Dependence

  • Absorption: scales roughly linearly with frequency (viscous losses).
  • Rayleigh scattering (d « λ): scales as f&sup4; — dominant in fine-grained materials at high frequency.
  • Stochastic scattering (d ~ λ): scales as f² — typical grain-noise regime in metals.
Rule of thumb: For carbon steel, α ≈ 0.5–2 dB/(mm·MHz) at 5 MHz depending on grain size. Coarse-grained castings can be 10× worse.

🔭 Beam Physics

Near Field (Fresnel Zone)

In the near field, beam intensity varies irregularly due to constructive/destructive interference. The near-field distance (Rayleigh distance) is:

N = D² / (4λ) = D² · f / (4c)

Inspection is generally more reliable in the far field (beyond N), or at the natural focus.

Far Field Beam Divergence

sin(θ½) ≈ 0.514 λ / D    (−6 dB half-angle)
sin(θ) = 1.2 λ / D        (first-null / Rayleigh criterion)

Focused Transducers

dspot ≈ 1.02 λ F / D = 1.02 F / (f-number)
Design trade-off: Tighter focus (smaller f-number) → better lateral resolution, but shorter depth of field. For thick parts, an unfocused or weakly focused beam may be preferred.

📈 Snell's Law & Mode Conversion

When an ultrasonic wave strikes a planar interface at oblique incidence, it simultaneously produces a refracted P-wave and a mode-converted S-wave in the second medium. Both obey Snell's law independently, each governed by its own wave speed:

sin(θi) / cL1 = sin(θL) / cL2 = sin(θS) / cS2

Because cL2 ≠ cS2, the two transmitted beams diverge in direction. In steel (cL = 5900 m/s, cS = 3200 m/s) the refracted P bends nearly twice as far from the normal as the mode-converted S.

Critical Angles

As θi increases, two thresholds mark where a transmitted wave reaches 90° (grazes the interface) and undergoes total internal reflection (TIR):

  • First critical angle (θc1): sin(θc1) = cL1 / cL2. The refracted P-wave undergoes TIR; for θi > θc1, only the mode-converted S transmits into the solid.
  • Second critical angle (θc2): sin(θc2) = cL1 / cS2. The mode-converted S also undergoes TIR; for θi > θc2, both waves are totally reflected and Rayleigh surface waves can be generated along the interface.

Angle-beam probes are designed to operate with the wedge angle strictly between θc1 and θc2, ensuring a single-mode (pure shear) beam enters the test piece.

Interface cL1 (m/s) cL2 / cS2 (m/s) θc1 θc2
Water → Steel 1480 5900 / 3200 14.5° 27.5°
Water → Aluminum 1480 6320 / 3130 13.5° 28.2°
Perspex → Steel 2730 5900 / 3200 27.6° 58.5°
Perspex → Aluminum 2730 6320 / 3130 25.6° 60.7°
NDE example — 45° shear weld probe (Perspex–steel):
Required wedge angle: sin(θwedge) = sin(45°) × cPerspex / cS-steel = 0.707 × 2730 / 3200 = 0.603 → θwedge ≈ 37°.
The wide Perspex–steel window (27.6° – 58.5°) means a single wedge material covers 45°, 60°, and 70° shear angles — each cut at a different face angle. For a 25 mm thick plate, one half-skip leg covers a surface distance of T × tan(45°) = 25 mm; a full V-path spans 50 mm, directly constraining probe placement during weld-root inspection.

Angle Beam Geometry

Depth = beam path × cos(θR)     Surface distance = beam path × sin(θR)

A full skip (V-path off the back wall) traverses a total beam path of 2T / cos(θR), where T is the part thickness and θR is the shear refraction angle. The quarter-, half-, and three-quarter-skip positions interrogate the near-surface, mid-thickness (back wall), and near-far-surface regions respectively — critical for full volumetric weld coverage per ASME and AWS D1.1.

📺 Signal Processing

The raw A-scan is a time-domain RF voltage waveform — a bipolar oscillating signal whose amplitude encodes reflector strength and whose timing encodes depth. Four processing steps convert that raw signal into a displayable, measurable, and imageable result.

Envelope Detection (Hilbert Transform)

Gated C-scans sample peak echo amplitude within a depth window. Sampling the raw RF signal directly produces a position-dependent artifact: a reflector sitting at a wave crest reads full amplitude; one at a zero-crossing reads zero — yet both are physically identical. The cure is the analytic signal:

z(t) = s(t) + j·H{s(t)}   →   |z(t)| = envelope

The Hilbert transform H{·} phase-shifts every frequency component by 90°, producing a quadrature imaginary part. The magnitude |z(t)| is a smooth, non-negative envelope that tracks peak echo amplitude regardless of RF phase. In practice it is computed via FFT → zero all negative-frequency bins → inverse FFT.

NDE example: Automated immersion UT of aerospace titanium forgings gates on the Hilbert envelope of the back-wall echo to flag embedded porosity. A −6 dB drop in gated amplitude marks a suspect region. Using raw RF instead would produce false positives every time the back wall shifts by a quarter wavelength relative to the gate centre — an artefact that no threshold setting can suppress.

Time Gain Compensation (TGC)

Attenuation decays echo amplitude exponentially with depth, so identical reflectors at different depths return unequal signals. TGC applies a programmable depth-dependent gain matched to the material's attenuation law:

GTGC(t) = exp(+α × c × t / 2)     [dB-linear ramp; compensates round-trip path loss]

In practice, α is measured on a step-wedge or DAC block and the ramp is set in dB/μs. A properly compensated A-scan shows equal amplitude for equal-size reflectors at all depths, making the C-scan color scale a direct indicator of reflectivity rather than a convolution of reflectivity and depth.

NDE example: Corrosion mapping of a 6–20 mm carbon steel pressure vessel wall at 5 MHz (α ≈ 1 dB/(mm·MHz)). Without TGC the back-wall echo from a 20 mm wall arrives 28 dB below that from a 6 mm wall (2 × 14 mm extra round-trip × 1 dB/mm). TGC recovers that loss; the C-scan then maps actual wall-thinning geometry rather than a depth-weighted echo map.

Pulse Compression (Chirp / Coded Excitation)

Conventional pulsed UT trades off resolution against SNR: shorter pulse → finer range resolution, but less energy transmitted → lower SNR. Pulse compression decouples these. A linear frequency-modulated (LFM) chirp of duration T and bandwidth B is transmitted; a matched filter (cross-correlation with the reference chirp) compresses the received signal to a pulse of length ≈ 1/B while preserving the full energy of the long transmission:

SNR gain = T × B    (time-bandwidth product)

Resolution equals that of a δ-pulse at bandwidth B; SNR equals that of a CW burst of duration T. The compressed sidelobe level must be managed (typically with amplitude weighting — Hann, Taylor — at the cost of slight resolution broadening).

NDE example: Air-coupled UT of CFRP composites suffers 60–80 dB insertion loss at each air–composite interface, burying conventional pulses in noise. A 50 μs chirp with B = 200 kHz yields T×B = 10 (+10 dB) — often the difference between resolving a delamination and noise-floor burial. Pulse compression is also standard in TOFD, where weak diffraction tip signals must be separated from lateral-wave and geometry arrivals.

SNR & Noise Sources

Three distinct noise mechanisms compete with flaw echoes and require different mitigation strategies:

Noise type Origin Frequency scaling Mitigation
Electronic noise Amplifier Johnson noise; preamplifier noise figure White (flat spectrum) Low-noise preamp; coherent averaging N shots → +10log₁₀(N) dB
Coherent grain noise ("grass") Backscatter from grain boundaries; coherent with transmitted pulse; cannot be averaged away ∝ f² (stochastic) or f&sup4; (Rayleigh, d « λ) Lower frequency; spatial compounding; FMC/TFM; frequency-dependent filtering
Geometric / structural noise Surface roughness, geometry re-entrant echoes, mode-converted arrivals Depends on geometry Time-gating; wedge design; surface preparation; TOFD lateral-wave subtraction
NDE example — austenitic weld inspection: 316L stainless steel welds contain columnar grains 100–500 μm wide. At 5 MHz in this microstructure, coherent grain noise sits only 10–15 dB below a 1 mm side-drilled hole reference — well within typical flaw-detection thresholds. Because grain noise is coherent, waveform averaging does not suppress it. The standard remedies are: (1) drop to 1–2 MHz to shift the stochastic scattering regime (accepting coarser resolution), or (2) use phased-array spatial compounding / FMC-TFM to decorrelate grain noise across apertures while keeping coherent flaw signals in phase.

🔈 Phased Arrays

A phased array transducer contains many small elements (typically 16–256) excited with programmable time delays, enabling electronic beam steering and focusing without physical movement.

Beam Steering

tn = (n · d · sinθ) / c

Applying delay tn to element n steers the beam to angle θ. Pitch d must be ≤ λ/2 to avoid grating lobes.

Sectorial Scan (S-scan)

The beam is electronically swept over a range of angles (e.g., 40°–70°) at a fixed focal depth, creating a fan-shaped image — extensively used for weld inspection.

Full Matrix Capture / Total Focusing Method (FMC/TFM)

Every transmit–receive element combination is recorded. TFM delay-and-sums this data to focus at every pixel in the image, achieving near-optimal resolution everywhere — the current gold standard for advanced array imaging.

Key advantage: Electronic scanning is orders of magnitude faster than mechanical scanning, enables rapid volumetric coverage, and allows real-time image reconstruction at inspection speed.

📏 Calibration & Reference Levels

Calibration establishes a quantitative reference so that any indication can be evaluated consistently regardless of depth or scan position.

Decibel Scale

ΔI (dB) = 20 log10(P2 / P1)
Quick conversions: +6 dB = ×2 amplitude  |  +20 dB = ×10  |  −6 dB = ×0.5  |  −20 dB = ×0.1  |  −40 dB = ×0.01

Distance Amplitude Correction (DAC)

DAC is a reference curve constructed by recording peak amplitude from identical reference reflectors (side-drilled holes or flat-bottom holes) at multiple depths. Any indication that reaches or exceeds the DAC curve is evaluated as a recordable indication — the curve has already accounted for amplitude decay with depth.

Reference Blocks & Standards

  • ASTM E127 / E428: Flat-bottom hole (FBH) blocks — standard for immersion calibration.
  • ASTM E1158: Distance-amplitude blocks — side-drilled holes at increasing depths.
  • ASME Basic Calibration Block: Required for pressure vessel and piping inspection (ASME Sec. V).
  • IIW V1 / V2 blocks: International Welding Institute blocks for velocity measurement and angle-probe index checks.
