⚡ The Piezoelectric Effect

All ultrasonic transducers used in NDE exploit the piezoelectric effect — the ability of certain materials to convert between electrical energy and mechanical vibration.

Common Piezoelectric Materials

Material Common name Strengths Limitations
Lead Zirconate Titanate PZT ceramic High sensitivity; most common in NDE Brittle; Curie temperature ~150–350°C
Polyvinylidene fluoride PVDF film Flexible; wideband; good for broadband detection Lower output; temperature limited
Lithium Niobate LiNbO&sub3; Stable at high temperatures (>300°C) More expensive; fragile
Quartz SiO&sub2; (natural/synthetic) Very stable; reproducible Lower piezoelectric coupling than PZT

🔌 Transducer Anatomy

A typical contact or immersion transducer has several layers, each with a specific function:

What each layer does

Backing materialHeavily damped epoxy or tungsten-loaded rubber. Absorbs any sound going rearward, shortening the pulse and improving axial resolution.
ElectrodesThin metal films (silver, gold) that apply the drive voltage across the crystal and capture the received signal.
Piezoelectric elementThe active layer. PZT disk or plate; its thickness sets the resonant frequency (t = λ/2; thinner = higher frequency).
Matching layer(s)One or two λ/4-thick layers with impedance between crystal (~30 MRayl) and water (~1.5 MRayl) or steel (~46 MRayl). Maximises energy transmission.
Wear plateHard protective layer (alumina, boron carbide, or epoxy) that physically contacts the test surface or couplant. Prevents crystal abrasion.
Bandwidth and resolution: The backing and matching layers together determine the transducer's −6 dB bandwidth. A heavily damped, broadband transducer (80–120% fractional bandwidth) produces very short pulses and fine axial resolution. Narrowband transducers sacrifice axial resolution for higher sensitivity.

🔎 Transducer Types — Overview

Different inspection problems call for different transducer configurations. Here is a summary of the major categories used in industrial NDE.

🔍

Contact (Straight-Beam)

Placed directly on the test surface with couplant. Generates longitudinal waves straight into the material. The workhorse for thickness measurement and lamination/inclusion detection.

Longitudinal wave 0.5–25 MHz Most common
📈

Angle-Beam (Wedge)

A wedge refracts the beam at Snell's law, converting to shear waves at a set angle (45°, 60°, 70°). Standard for weld inspection and crack detection in welds, pipes, and rails.

Shear / Rayleigh wave 1–10 MHz Weld inspection
💦

Immersion

Both transducer and part submerged in water. The water column couples the beam and allows precise computer-controlled scanning. Common for C-scan imaging and complex geometry parts.

Water-coupled 1–50 MHz C-scan / production
🔂

Dual-Element (TR)

Separate transmitter and receiver elements in one housing, inclined slightly toward each other. Eliminates the near-surface dead zone; ideal for corrosion mapping of thin sections.

Pulse-echo variant 2–10 MHz Corrosion mapping
⚡

EMAT

Electromagnetic Acoustic Transducer. Non-contact generation via Lorentz force and magnetostriction. No couplant needed; works on rough, coated, or hot surfaces.

Non-contact Shear or Lamb waves No couplant
🌬

Air-Coupled

Non-contact with air as couplant. Very low impedance mismatch managed by matching layers and signal averaging. Used for composites, foam, ceramics, and paper.

Non-contact 0.05–5 MHz Composites
🔭

Phased Array (PAUT)

Multi-element array (16–256 elements) with programmable delay laws. Electronic beam steering, focusing, and sectorial scans without mechanical movement. Current industry standard for weld and structural inspection.

All wave modes 1–15 MHz Gold standard
🔳

TOFD Probes

Time-of-Flight Diffraction uses two angled probes straddling a weld. Measures diffracted signals from crack tips, giving very accurate depth sizing independent of crack orientation.

Angled-beam pair 2–10 MHz Weld / crack sizing

Contact Transducers (Straight-Beam)

The simplest and most widely used configuration: the transducer face is pressed against the test surface (with a thin layer of couplant), and longitudinal waves travel perpendicular to the surface.

Key Design Parameters

Frequency1–25 MHz. Higher frequency for fine-grained metals; lower for coarse-grained castings or plastics.
Element diameterTypically 6–25 mm. Larger diameter → narrower divergence in far field, longer near-field zone.
Near-field lengthN = D² / (4λ). Inspection in the near field is unreliable due to interference — plan scan depth accordingly.
Bandwidth60–120% fractional bandwidth. Broadband = short pulse = good axial resolution.

Applications

  • Thickness measurement (corrosion inspection, wall-loss)
  • Laminar flaw detection (delaminations, inclusions)
  • Bond integrity testing
  • Velocity measurement for material characterisation
Couplant required. A thin liquid or gel film (water, glycerin, or commercial gel) is needed between the transducer face and the part to displace air and allow energy transfer. Without couplant, nearly 100% of energy is reflected at the air gap.

Immersion Transducers

The transducer is aimed at the part from a distance, with water filling the gap. The water column acts as a perfect, consistent couplant and a variable focal length.

Advantages over contact

  • No transducer wear; uniform coupling across a scan
  • Precise computer-controlled XY positioning
  • Focused transducers can be tuned by adjusting water path (focal distance)
  • Enables high-frequency (up to 50 MHz) inspection with minimal surface noise

Types

Flat (unfocused)Natural cylindrical beam; used when the full thickness must be interrogated.
Spherically focusedLens or curved crystal focuses the beam to a point — best lateral resolution at the focus depth.
Cylindrically focusedFocuses in one plane only; used to interrogate a line rather than a point.
Water path calculation: To place the focal spot at depth d inside a material of velocity cm, the required water path W is approximated by W ≈ F − d × (cw / cm), where F is the transducer's focal length in water and cw ≈ 1480 m/s.

Dual-Element (TR) Transducers

A TR (transmit-receive) transducer houses two separate elements side by side in one housing, acoustically isolated from each other, and angled slightly toward one another ("roof angle"). The transmitter fires; the receiver picks up reflections.

Why use it?

  • No dead zone: The transmitter is electrically isolated from the receiver, so near-surface echoes are not masked by the main bang.
  • Corrosion mapping: Ideal for measuring remaining wall thickness in pipes and vessels with internal corrosion.
  • Rough surfaces: Less sensitive to surface condition variations.
Limitation: The crossed-beam geometry creates a pseudo-focal zone (the intersection depth) that varies with the roof angle. Sensitivity is not flat with depth — the beam is optimised for a specific depth range set by the design.

Angle-Beam Transducers (Wedge Probes)

A standard straight-beam transducer is mounted on a plastic wedge. When sound enters the wedge at an oblique angle, Snell's law converts it to shear waves in the test material at a specific refraction angle. Common angles are 45°, 60°, and 70° (shear wave in the material).

sin(θ2) / cS = sin(θ1) / cwedge

Applications

  • Weld inspection (fusion zone flaws, porosity, lack of penetration)
  • Pipe seam welds and circumferential scanning
  • Rail inspection
  • Turbine blade root inspection
Index point and beam exit: The "index point" is the precise location on the wedge face where the beam exits. Always mark the index point and measure from it when calculating reflector position. It changes with wear — check regularly on a calibration block (IIW V1 or V2).

EMAT — Electromagnetic Acoustic Transducers

EMATs generate and receive ultrasound using electromagnetic forces rather than piezoelectric vibration. A coil carrying RF current sits in a static magnetic field above the part. The Lorentz force drives surface currents in the metal, which in turn drives acoustic waves. No couplant is required.

Mechanism

  • Lorentz force (non-ferromagnetic metals): RF current in coil → eddy currents in part skin depth → Lorentz force from static B-field → acoustic wave.
  • Magnetostriction (ferromagnetic metals): Oscillating magnetic field changes the shape of the material at the atomic level, generating stress waves.

Wave modes available with EMAT

SH (shear-horizontal)Unique to EMAT — not easily generated with piezoelectrics. Useful for corrosion and guided wave inspection.
Rayleigh (surface waves)Meander-coil configuration; inspects surface and near-surface regions.
Lamb wavesPlate/shell guided waves for long-range screening.

Trade-offs

Advantage: Non-contact; works on hot (~500°C), moving, or coated surfaces.
Limitation: Signal-to-noise ratio is 20–40 dB lower than comparable piezoelectric probes; requires stronger magnetic fields (permanent magnets or electromagnets) and more signal averaging. Only works on electrically conductive materials.

Air-Coupled Transducers

Air-coupled UT eliminates liquid couplant entirely. The impedance mismatch between air (~400 Rayl) and most solids (~1–46 MRayl) means only about 10−4% of energy transmits — so specialised designs are needed.

Enabling technologies

  • Matching layers: Multiple λ/4 layers step the impedance from the crystal down toward air.
  • PVDF and capacitive micromachined (CMUT) elements: Low-impedance transduction that couples better to air.
  • Signal averaging / coded excitation: Many averages or long-chirp pulses compensate for low SNR.

Applications

  • Carbon-fiber composite inspection (delaminations, impact damage)
  • Foam, honeycomb, paper, and cardboard structures
  • Ceramic and glass panels
  • Situations where liquid contamination is unacceptable

Phased Array Transducers (PAUT)

A phased array probe contains many individual piezoelectric elements (typically 16, 32, 64, or 128) arranged in a linear, annular, or 2D matrix. By firing elements with programmable time delays (focal laws), the beam can be steered, focused, and swept electronically.

Scan modes

ModeDescriptionTypical use
E-scan (linear) A fixed aperture is stepped along the array; same focal law at each position. Equivalent to a mechanically scanned single element; fast coverage
S-scan (sectorial) Beam angle swept over a range (e.g., 40°–70°) using the full array; fan-shaped image. Weld volumetric inspection — current standard
FMC / TFM Every element fires in sequence while all others receive. Post-process TFM focuses at every point. Highest image quality; flaw characterisation; research

Key specification parameters

Number of elements16–256. More elements → wider steering range and finer beam control.
Element pitch (d)Must be ≤ λ/2 to avoid grating lobes (false beams at off-axis angles).
Center frequency1–15 MHz for structural NDE; up to 50 MHz for medical and microelectronics.
ApertureActive element count × pitch. Larger aperture → narrower beam in far field.
Why phased arrays dominate modern NDE: A single probe replaces an entire set of angle-beam probes. Inspection of a full weld volume takes seconds instead of minutes. Real-time imaging with TFM delivers near-CT-quality cross-sections in the field.

🎯 How to Choose a Transducer

No single transducer works best for every situation. Use this guide as a starting point:

Inspection need Recommended type Frequency guidance
Wall thickness / corrosion Contact straight-beam or dual-element (TR) 2–10 MHz
Weld inspection (manual) Angle-beam wedge (45°/60°/70° shear) 2–5 MHz
Weld inspection (encoded) Phased array (S-scan) + TOFD 2–10 MHz
Composite delamination Immersion or contact straight-beam; air-coupled if no couplant 5–15 MHz
Large structure long-range screening EMAT or Guided Wave (low-frequency ring array) 30–300 kHz
High-resolution imaging, electronics Immersion focused, high-frequency 15–100 MHz
Hot surface or no-couplant required EMAT or air-coupled Application-specific
Frequency vs. resolution vs. penetration: Higher frequency = smaller wavelength = finer resolution, but more attenuation = less depth penetration. Start with the minimum frequency that gives you the resolution you need. If attenuation is too high, step down in frequency.
